00 — The brief

What we're trying to accomplish

The official Bard notes ask for instruments to become central to the class rather than optional flourishes: more damage songs, more songs that genuinely require an instrument, and less reliance on permanent, set-and-forget charm. The hard part isn't adding songs — it's making the instrument in your hands matter moment to moment, and giving the bard real decisions, without letting any of it spiral into runaway power.

This proposal adds two stacking systems — Attunement and Resonance — both held in check by hyperbolic scaling (diminishing returns) so the numbers stay bounded no matter how hard they're stacked. On top of that sit four instrument families that each reshape the same underlying bonus in a different way, so choosing wind, percussion, string, or brass is choosing a playstyle, not just a stat stick.

It is, admittedly, a fair amount of machinery. The wager is that the depth of choice is what makes a bard fun to actually play — and that the diminishing-returns math is the safety rail that lets us offer that depth without breaking the class over our knee.

01 — The core loop

Cadence, and the game tick

A bard keeps a song alive by replaying it about every 3 seconds. The song's effect — damage, a heal, a tick of mana — lands on the world's 6‑second tick, the heartbeat that everything else in the world already runs on. Two casts per tick is the rhythm the whole system sits on.

The one change we'd actually recommend

Today a song's effect tends to count time from the moment you cast it. We'd recommend instead that bard effects resolve on the game tick — sampled on the global heartbeat rather than on a private per-cast timer.

The reason is that everything below — how much a song has "warmed up" — gets read at a single, predictable instant. That turns timing into a decision instead of a quiet dice-roll on whether your cast happened to line up with the tick. It also lets the bard's effects breathe with the rest of the encounter's rhythm.

Not a hard requirement. Every system in this document works just as well if effects keep counting from each cast. Only the feel of the timing changes — anchoring to the shared heartbeat is the version we think reads cleanest, but the time-from-cast version is a perfectly fine fallback.
02 — The global floor

Attunement

Every time the bard finishes a cast — any song — Attunement rises. It climbs a hyperbolic curve toward a cap set by the instrument you're holding, and it applies to every song you play as a baseline bonus: a floor under everything. Think of it as the bard warming up with the instrument. It carries across song changes and only steps back down if you swap to a different instrument.

# Attunement — global, builds on every cast attunement = cap × n / (k + n) # n = casts performed · k tunes how fast it saturates

Because the curve is hyperbolic, the first casts give the biggest gains and each later cast gives a little less. The bonus approaches the cap and never punches through it — which is the whole trick to keeping it safe.

03 — Per‑song depth

Resonance

Where Attunement is global, Resonance belongs to the single song you're currently sustaining. Each consecutive cast of the same song builds Resonance on top of the Attunement floor, reaching toward the instrument's higher Resonance cap. The instant a song drops — you let it lapse, or you tried to juggle too many at once — that song's Resonance resets to the floor.

bonus = attunement + resonance # capped at the resonance cap value = base × (1 + bonus)

This is the heart of the design: a breadth‑versus‑depth gradient. Twist fewer songs and each is recast more often, so each carries higher Resonance. Twist many and each is recast rarely, so each sits low. Three songs played tightly out‑resonate six songs played sloppily. And a bard greedily holding more songs than the 3‑second cadence can support will watch them fall off and reset — an honest, self‑correcting penalty for over‑reach rather than a hard cap on song count.

04 — Four tools, one bonus reshaped

The instrument families

All four feed the same Attunement / Resonance engine — but each bends it a different way. They sort along two axes: how the bonus ramps (quick and front‑loaded vs. slow and steady), and which playstyle they reward (twisting a wide rotation vs. committing to one effect).

Percussiondrum · burst

Front‑loaded. Resonance ramps hyperbolically — quick to most of its value — and the drum adds a small pulse on every refresh. The tool for short, sharp windows where you want the payoff now.

attune 15%resonance 25%pulse +5%/refresh
Windbreadth · speed

Breadth through speed. Wind casts faster as Attunement builds — the cadence shrinks with your attunement. Tuned so that, fully attuned, it buys exactly one more held song than the baseline. A wind bard twists a wider rotation without dropping anything.

attune 15%resonance 25%cadence 3s × (1 − attune)
Stringbreadth · uptime

Breadth through duration. String songs last longer as Attunement builds. Also tuned for one more held song at full attunement — and the longer duration means each effect reaches further across the heartbeat, which is what you want for multi‑target coverage.

attune 17%resonance 25%duration 18s × (1 + attune)
Brasshorn · commitment

Depth and commitment. Brass advances Resonance a constant amount every cast rather than front‑loading, with a kick on the very first cast and the highest caps of any family. Deliberately the slow burn: it overtakes the front‑loaded families only if you stay committed. Its magnitude is meant for single‑effect songs.

attune 20%resonance 35%advance +3%/cast
No instrument — singing only. The baseline: no Resonance, just the bard's voice. Everything above is the upside of actually playing an instrument.
05 — A finding from the math

Why Brass lives on single effects, not twisted DoTs

Running the numbers settles an old question. A five‑DoT twist always out‑damages a single sustained DoT on one target — it isn't close. Even a heavily‑stacked single damage song reaches only a fraction of what the twist delivers, and bringing them to parity would need a bonus so large it would break everything else.

So single‑DoT "dedication" is intentionally not a single‑target DPS path — and that's fine, because it frees Brass's big magnitude to point at the things that are single‑effect by nature: a heal, a charm, a hate spike, or a buff held on one target. Committing to one song and ramping it hard is the payoff, on the songs where ramping one thing is the whole point — and the Serenade ability below is the tool built for exactly that moment.

06 — The safety rail

Hyperbolic scaling — why the Bard doesn't blow up

Every bonus here rides a diminishing‑returns curve of the same shape: value / (k + value). The first stacks are strong, each additional stack is worth a little less, and the total asymptotes to a ceiling it can never cross.

# diminishing returns — same shape everywhere effective = value / (k + value) # approaches 1, never reaches it # same-type effects add inside a bracket; brackets then scale

Same‑type effects add together inside a shared bracket, and the bracket scales the value — so two sources of the same bonus don't multiply into something absurd. That's the point: we can hand the bard a deep stack of knobs precisely because the output is bounded by construction. The depth doesn't become raw power; it becomes choice.

07 — Two ways to break the rhythm

Crescendo & Serenade

Everything to here is passive accumulation — warm up, hold your songs, let Resonance climb. Two activated abilities let the bard spike that accumulation on demand: one overcharges the whole performance, the other pours it into a single target.

CrescendoActivated · overcharge

For 18 seconds, your Attunement is pinned at 50% regardless of the instrument in your hands — well above any instrument's normal cap — so every song you play rides a 50% floor. When the window closes, Attunement settles straight back to its normal accumulated value.

This is the bard's burst button: a true overcharge rather than a fast warm‑up. Because 50% sits above even Brass's ceiling, Crescendo lifts you past a height no instrument reaches on its own — and it works the same whether you're holding a horn or singing bare‑handed. Line it up with your hardest‑hitting rotation and let it rip.

# Crescendo — activated, instrument-agnostic attunement = 50% for 18s # overrides the usual ceiling # reverts to normal attunement the moment it expires
SerenadeActivated · single target

Activate Serenade and your next song becomes a 6‑second performance aimed at one target, applying once per second — six rapid ticks, in the spirit of how bandaging channels today.

It inherits everything. The song's current Resonance and Attunement scale each of the six ticks, Percussion's front‑loaded pulse still fires, and Wind's cast speed and String's duration still shape it. So the instrument you main colors how your Serenade plays — brass lands heavy and steady, percussion front‑loads, string stretches the channel, wind quickens it. This is the focus‑fire tool: ramp a song to high Resonance, then dump it into one target in a tight burst — the mechanical home for a charm lock, a hate spike, a clutch heal, or a focused debuff.

# Serenade — activated, channels on one target next song → 6s channel, 1 tick / second # 6 ticks, single target # each tick scales with resonance + attunement # drum pulse · wind cast-speed · string duration all still apply

Together they compound: a Serenade fired inside a Crescendo is six single‑target ticks riding a 50% floor — the bard's biggest single moment. (Both abilities live in the design here; the interactive model focuses on the passive Attunement/Resonance loop and doesn't simulate them yet.)

08 — The point of all of it

The decisions this creates

Every system above exists to put a choice in front of the bard. A few of the ones that emerge:

Foresight

Ramp the heal

Read the incoming damage, start the healing hymn early, and hold it so its Resonance is near cap by the time the tank actually gets hit — trading a few seconds of anticipation for a much larger heal at the moment it counts.

Focus fire

Serenade a target

Ramp a song to high Resonance, then fire Serenade to pour it into one target as a six‑tick burst — and stack it under Crescendo for the bard's single biggest hit. The focused payoff the damage math deliberately leaves room for.

Breadth

Twist wide or play tight

Spread across many songs for coverage — Wind and String let you hold one more — or twist a tight three for higher Resonance on each. The gradient is yours to slide along.

Read the fight

Pick the instrument

Drum for a burst window, brass for a long commitment, wind or string for a wide rotation. The instrument in your hand becomes a real, per‑encounter call.

09 — See it move

The interactive model

Numbers on a page only go so far, so this all comes with a live model you can poke at. Build a rotation, set a level and a fight length, dial each instrument's caps, and watch it play out.

  • Cumulative delivery for all instruments at once — damage, healing, mana, or combined.
  • A cast timeline showing exactly when each song is held, when it drops, and how bright its Resonance is — hover any bar for its next‑tick value.
  • A per‑song bonus curve with the Attunement floor drawn underneath.
  • A starting‑attunement slider, to model an already‑warmed‑up fight instead of a cold open.

Try a seven‑song twist: most instruments sawtooth as songs fall off and reset, while wind and string hold the line once attuned. That single picture is the whole breadth‑versus‑depth argument.

10 — Over to you

Open questions & an invitation

This is a lot of machinery, and it may genuinely be more than the class wants to carry — that's a fair critique and I'd rather hear it than not. But the depth of choice is, to me, exactly what would make this bard a joy to play, and the hyperbolic scaling is the rail that keeps that depth from turning into raw power.

Suggestions, holes, and "you've overcomplicated this" are all welcome. Read the plan, open the model, break it, and tell me where it's wrong.
